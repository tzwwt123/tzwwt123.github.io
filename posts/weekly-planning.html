Weekly Planning Prompt for ChatGPT

Updated July 23, 2026 • Productivity workflow • 6 min read

Use this weekly planning prompt for ChatGPT when tasks, meetings, and deadlines live in different places. It turns a messy list into a realistic plan by working backward from real outcomes and calling out overload instead of filling every empty calendar slot.

Best use cases

  • Choosing the few outcomes that should exist by Friday.
  • Creating a weekly schedule around meetings and focus time.
  • Finding overloaded days before the week starts.
  • Turning goals into daily next actions.
  • Deciding what to delay, shorten, delegate, or delete.

The workflow

  1. Write the results you want to have by the end of the week.
  2. Paste your task list, fixed meetings, deadlines, and available work hours.
  3. Add your best focus times, low-energy times, and personal constraints.
  4. Ask ChatGPT to build the week backward from those outcomes.
  5. Make it mark overloaded days, unclear tasks, and work that should move out of this week.

What to prepare first

  • End-of-week outcomes, not just activities.
  • Fixed meetings, appointments, and hard deadlines.
  • Tasks that need deep focus versus quick admin.
  • Energy pattern: best focus hours, low-energy windows, and days likely to be interrupted.
  • Anything that cannot move, such as school pickup, client calls, travel, or support coverage.

Copy this prompt

Help me plan a realistic week by working backward from the results I need by Friday. End-of-week outcomes: [what should be finished, decided, sent, reviewed, or learned by Friday] Current week: [dates, timezone, and any public holiday or day off] Task list: [paste all tasks, even messy ones] Fixed meetings and appointments: [paste meetings, calls, personal commitments, support coverage, or travel] Deadlines: [paste hard deadlines and soft target dates] Available work hours: [available hours by day] Capacity limit: [maximum realistic deep-work hours and admin hours per day] Focus and energy constraints: [best focus times, low-energy times, likely interruptions, personal commitments] Decision rules: [what matters most if the week is too full, such as client deadline, revenue work, health, family, or manager priority] Rules: - Do not create a perfect-looking schedule if the workload is too large. - Protect focus blocks for work that needs real thinking. - Put quick admin, email, or cleanup work into low-energy windows. - If an outcome is vague, ask what "done" means instead of guessing. - Mark anything that should be delayed, shortened, delegated, deleted, or moved to a later week. Return: 1. The 3-5 outcomes that matter most this week 2. A day-by-day plan with focus blocks and admin blocks 3. A capacity check that says whether the plan fits the available time 4. Tasks to delay, shorten, delegate, or delete 5. Overloaded days, missing information, and unrealistic assumptions 6. A short Monday morning checklist 7. A Friday review checklist to confirm what actually got finished

Test input

End-of-week outcomes: - Send the client deck by Thursday - Finish payroll review by Friday noon - Draft the June newsletter - Clear five overdue customer replies Task list: - Client deck outline, data cleanup, final slides, review with manager - Payroll spreadsheet check - Newsletter topic ideas and first draft - Customer replies - Expense report - Update project board Fixed meetings: - Monday 10:00 team meeting - Tuesday 14:00 client call - Wednesday 09:30 dentist - Thursday 15:00 manager review Available work hours: Monday 9-5, Tuesday 9-4, Wednesday 11-5, Thursday 9-5, Friday 9-3 Focus constraints: Best focus time is morning. After 15:00 I can handle admin but not complex writing.

Good output should include

  • A short list of true outcomes, not a giant task dump.
  • Focus blocks placed where your energy is strongest.
  • Admin work grouped into lighter parts of the day.
  • A capacity check before the final schedule.
  • A clear list of work that does not fit this week.
  • A Friday review so next week's plan starts from reality, not wishful thinking.

If the plan is still too full

This plan still looks too crowded. Please audit it again. Check: 1. Which outcomes can realistically be finished this week? 2. Which tasks are only nice-to-have? 3. Which tasks depend on someone else? 4. Which day has too much deep-focus work? 5. What should I delay, shorten, delegate, or delete? Rewrite the plan so it leaves buffer time and protects the most important outcome.

Common mistake

Do not ask for a perfect schedule if your task list is impossible. Ask ChatGPT to point out overload. A useful plan should tell you what not to do this week.
